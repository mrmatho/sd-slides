# Evaluation Criteria

::content::

Evaluation criteria are the standards by which you evaluate your designs. They are based on:

- Functional and Non-Functional Requirements
- User Needs
- Constraints

We use criteria that are **testable**, **measurable** and **objective** (as much as possible) to allow us to make decisions based on evidence rather than opinions.

***Your evaluation criteria are used twice: once to select your design and at the end of the project to evaluate the final product.***

# Evaluation Criteria

::content::

**Evaluation criteria can be categorised into:**
- Efficiency criteria
- Effectiveness criteria

<SpeechBubble color="purple-light">

**Efficiency** measures the resources used to achieve a goal, such as time, effort, or cost. Many of the non-functional requirements relate directly to efficiency criteria, such as performance, scalability, and maintainability. Efficiency criteria are relevant for both the development process and the final product. 

</SpeechBubble>

<SpeechBubble color="blue-light">

**Effectiveness** measures how well a design achieves its intended purpose. Many of the functional requirements relate directly to effectiveness criteria, such as usability, accessibility, and user satisfaction.

</SpeechBubble>

# Poor Evaluation Criteria

::content::

<v-clicks depth="2">

- "The design should look good"
  - **Improve to:** "The design should use a consistent colour scheme and typography to create a visually appealing interface"
- "The design should be easy to use"
  - **Improve to:** "The design should allow users to complete common tasks (eg. placing an order, searching for a product, etc.) within 3 clicks"
  - **Improve to:** "The design shows clear labels and instructions for how to use the search feature"
- "The design should be fast"
  - **Improve to:** "The design should allow the search results to load within 2 seconds"

</v-clicks>

# Performing the Evaluation

::content::

Because of the nature of our designs, not all evaluation criteria will be relevant for all design ideas. 

For example, if one of your evaluation criteria is "The design should allow users to complete common tasks within 3 clicks", then you would only evaluate this criterion for designs that include a user interface.

**Process:**

1. For each design idea, determine which criteria are relevant.
2. For each relevant design idea, determine whether it meets the criteria or not (eg. yes/no for simple criteria, a score out of 5 where more judgment is needed)
3. Document the results in a clear and organised way (eg. a table, a spreadsheet, etc.) to allow for easy comparison between designs
4. Select the most promising designs based on the evaluation results
5. Highlight any particular strengths in other ideas that should be incorporated.

# Justifying your design decisions

::content::

When justifying your design decisions, you should refer to the evaluation criteria and the results of your evaluation.

<SpeechBubble color="orange-light">

"I chose to develop Sketch 1 further because it met all of the relevant criteria, while Sketch 2 did not meet Criterion 1. This matches wel with the web-based approach, which performed well on relevant criteria. The moodboard with navy, white and orange colours scored the highest on Criterion 2, so I will incorporate that colour scheme into the final design."

</SpeechBubble>

# What are you going to submit? 

::content::

- **Your designs**: mood board(s), sketches, brainstorming ideas, mind map(s). Scans or photos are fine.
- **Your evaluation criteria (7-10)**
- **Your evaluation results** (eg. the table you created to document the evaluation results)
- **A written justification** of which design ideas you choose to develop further and why
  - Which design you chose as your primary final design
  - Which criteria were most important in making your decision, and how each design performed against those criteria
  - Any particular strengths in other ideas that should be incorporated into the final design

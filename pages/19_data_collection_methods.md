# Survey

::content::

- Collecting data by asking pre-defined questions to a group of people.
- Online, face-to-face, or telephone surveys (you'd obviously use online or face-to-face)
- Questions can be open-ended or closed-ended:
  - Open-ended: "What do you think about our product?"
  - Closed-ended: "How satisfied are you with our product? (1-5)"
- Advantages: Quick, cost-effective, can reach a large audience.
- Disadvantages: May not provide in-depth insights, response bias, low response rates.

**Use surveys to quickly and efficiently gather lots of data from a large group of people, especially when you need quantitative data or want to identify trends.**

# Interview

::content::

- Collecting data through direct, one-on-one conversations.
- Can be structured (pre-defined questions) or unstructured (open-ended).
- Advantages: In-depth insights, allows for follow-up questions, builds rapport.
- Disadvantages: Time-consuming, may require skilled interviewers, potential for interviewer bias.

**Use interviews to explore topics in depth, from one or two people who have valuable insights or experiences to share.**

# Observation

::content::

- Collecting data by watching and recording behaviors or events.
- Can be targetted (watching for very specific things) or general (watching for anything interesting).
- Advantages: Provides real-world insights, can capture non-verbal behavior, less reliance on self-reporting.
- Disadvantages: Time-consuming, potential for observer bias, may not capture internal thoughts or motivations.

**Use observation to understand how people behave in real-world settings, especially when you want to capture things that people might not realise they do.**

# Report

::content::

- Collecting data from existing documents, records, or databases.
- Can include things like sales reports, customer feedback, or social media analytics.
- Advantages: Low effort (if the data exists), can provide historical insights, can be quantitative or qualitative.
- Disadvantages: Data has to exist and be aligned with your program's needs. 

**Use reports when there is existing data that can provide relevant insights for your program.**

# But how will this help me gather requirements for my project?

::content::

The SAT Criteria requires you to collect data to inform your functional and non-functional requirements, constraints and project scope. 

**You need to:**

- Identify where you can collect data from - who can you ask? What can you observe? What reports can you access?
- Pick which data collection method is **least** valuable for your project - you need to use 3 methods for the highest marks in the SAT Criteria.
- Craft smart questions to ask in surveys and interviews. Have a clear plan for observations (including data recording) and identify any available reports that can provide relevant data.
- ***For each data collection method:*** 
  - Collect data
  - Identify key points you found from that data
  - Make sure those key points get reflected in your requirements, constraints and project scope.
